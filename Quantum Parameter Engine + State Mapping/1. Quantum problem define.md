## The Big Idea (Plain English First)

Ship hulls are made of DH36 grade steel — which is mostly iron (Fe). When seawater sits on iron, a chemical reaction happens: iron atoms lose electrons to oxygen atoms. The result is rust — iron oxide (Fe₂O₃). Once rust forms, it keeps growing and eating into the metal.

The question we're trying to answer with quantum computing is: **exactly HOW fast does this electron movement happen?**

The answer depends on quantum mechanical properties of the Fe₂O₃ molecule — specifically its energy levels and how easily electrons can tunnel through the oxide layer. These properties come from solving the molecule's quantum equations.

Traditional engineering uses rough estimates for corrosion rates based on lab experiments. VQE gives us a rate constant (`k`) calculated from first principles — actual physics — which is far more accurate for specific materials in specific seawater conditions.

---

## Why Not Just Use Classical Computers?

The Schrödinger equation for Fe₂O₃ involves **142 electrons** all interacting with each other simultaneously. The number of quantum states grows as 2^N — so 142 electrons means 2^142 possible states. That's a number with 43 digits.

No classical computer, not even the largest supercomputer, can store or process that.

Quantum computers handle this naturally because qubits can exist in superposition — representing many states at once. That's the whole point of this quantum layer.

---

## Active Space Approximation — Why We Don't Need All 142 Electrons

Here's the key trick: not all 142 electrons are equally important for corrosion. Core electrons (close to the nucleus) are tightly bound and don't participate in chemical reactions. Only the outer electrons in certain orbitals actually affect how corrosion happens.

We pick the chemically important electrons and orbitals — called the **active space** — and do the quantum calculation only on those. This is called **CAS (Complete Active Space)**.

For Fe₂O₃, the active space is defined as:

```
Active space: CAS(10, 8)
  → 10 active electrons
  → 8 active orbitals (4 occupied + 4 virtual)

Why these orbitals?
  → Fe 3d orbitals (5 orbitals, partially filled): dominate corrosion chemistry
  → O 2p orbitals (3 orbitals): form bonds with Fe
  → These are the orbitals involved in electron transfer during oxidation

Electrons excluded from active space:
  → 132 core + semi-core electrons (1s, 2s, 2p for Fe; 1s for O)
  → These are captured via effective core potential (ECP) approximation

Spin state: antiferromagnetic
  → Fe³⁺ ions in Fe₂O₃ have opposing spins
  → Net spin = 0 (singlet effective state for VQE)
  → Validated against DFT literature: Fe₂O₃ is antiferromagnetic below 960K ✓
```

This reduces the problem from 2^142 states to 2^16 = 65,536 states. Still large, but tractable on quantum hardware.

## The Hamiltonian — What Are We Actually Solving?

The Hamiltonian (H) is a mathematical way of writing "total energy of the system". When we solve:

```
H|ψ⟩ = E|ψ⟩
```

We get the ground state energy **E₀** — the lowest energy the molecule can have. That energy tells us everything about how the molecule behaves, including how fast it corrodes.

In second quantisation (the form quantum computers understand), the Hamiltonian is:

```
H = Σᵢⱼ hᵢⱼ · a⁺ᵢaⱼ  +  (1/2) Σᵢⱼₖₗ gᵢⱼₖₗ · a⁺ᵢa⁺ₖaₗaⱼ
```

Breaking this down:

- **hᵢⱼ** = one-electron integrals. How much energy does one electron have moving from orbital j to orbital i? Includes kinetic energy and attraction to the nucleus.
- **gᵢⱼₖₗ** = two-electron integrals. When two electrons are in the same space, they repel each other. This term captures that repulsion.
- **a⁺ and a** = creation and annihilation operators. Mathematical tools meaning "add an electron here" and "remove an electron there".

These integrals (hᵢⱼ and gᵢⱼₖₗ) are computed **classically by PySCF** before the quantum computer ever runs. The quantum computer's job is to find the lowest energy state — not to compute the integrals.

## Matrix Representation

For CAS(10,8), the Hamiltonian lives in a Hilbert space of dimension:

```
dim = C(8,5) × C(8,5) = 56 × 56 = 3,136    [for 5 α-electrons, 5 β-electrons]
```

In practice we work in the Slater determinant basis. The Hamiltonian is represented as a **28×28 sparse matrix** (after spin symmetry reduction).

This matrix is the direct input to VQE. It is computed and stored by PySCF, verified against published Fe₂O₃ DFT calculations, then handed to the quantum pipeline.
